# The Great Inflation Divergence of January 2026: Why Core PPI Exploded +0.8% While CPI Stayed Cool

**Subtitle:** PPI and CPI told two very different stories last month. Here’s what it means for the Fed, consumers, and the rest of 2026.

On February 13, 2026, the Consumer Price Index (CPI) for January came in soft and reassuring: headline +0.2% m/m (below the expected +0.3%), core +0.3% m/m (in line), headline y/y +2.4%. Markets liked it. Two weeks later, on February 27, the Producer Price Index (PPI) landed like a bomb: headline +0.5% m/m (vs expected +0.3%), but the real shock was the **+0.8% month-over-month jump in final demand services less food, energy, and trade services** — the sharpest monthly gain in services since July 2025. Dow futures dropped ~700 points intraday. What happened?

## 1. Core PPI vs. Core CPI: A Side-by-Side Comparison

| Aspect                      | Core CPI (ex food & energy)                          | Core PPI (Final Demand less food, energy & trade services) |
|-----------------------------|------------------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------|
| Measures                    | Prices consumers pay at retail                       | Prices producers receive from other businesses (wholesale) |
| Perspective                 | End consumer                                         | Business-to-business / early pipeline                      |
| Includes imports?           | Yes                                                  | No                                                         |
| Includes third-party payments? | No (only direct household spending)               | Yes (e.g. employer-provided health plans)                  |
| Services coverage           | Very broad (rent, medical, education, etc.)          | Partial (excludes most government/non-market services)     |
| Typical lead/lag            | Lagging indicator                                    | Often leads CPI by 3–9 months                              |
| January 2026 m/m            | +0.3% (as expected)                                  | Services component +0.8% (big surprise); overall core ~+0.3–0.4% range |
| January 2026 y/y            | +2.5%                                                | ~+3.6% (highest in ~10 months)                             |

Key BLS note: “There are many reasons why the PPI and CPI may diverge.” CPI captures retail + imports + taxes; PPI captures upstream margins + B2B transactions.

## 2. Why the +0.8% Services Jump Was Such a Surprise

Consensus forecasts had core PPI services in the +0.3% to +0.6% range. The actual **+0.8% m/m** reading (on the core services ex-food/energy/trade measure) was well above that — and it drove almost the entire beat.

Markets had just digested a dovish CPI print and were pricing in ~60–70% odds of a June Fed cut. The PPI release flipped sentiment: June cut probability dropped sharply, 2-year Treasury yields rose ~10 bps intraday, and “higher for longer” talk returned.

## 3. What Drove the Divergence: The Smoking Gun = Trade Services Margins

The BLS breakdown tells the story:

- **Final demand services** overall: +0.8% m/m  
- **Final demand trade services margins**: +2.5% m/m (the biggest single contributor)  
- Within that: **professional & commercial equipment wholesaling margins** surged **+14.4%** m/m — accounting for >20% of the total services increase  
- Other notable widenings: apparel/footwear/accessories retailing, chemicals & plastics wholesaling, health & beauty aids retailing, food & alcohol wholesaling/retailing

Translation: Wholesalers and retailers **expanded profit margins** rather than simply passing through higher input costs. This is textbook “pricing power” reappearing at the wholesale level — precisely the kind of persistent services inflation that keeps the Fed nervous.

CPI, by contrast, looked tame because:
- Gasoline fell -3.2% m/m (big downward pull on headline)
- Shelter inflation continued to slow (lagging 12-month rents still decelerating)
- Margin expansion and early tariff effects had not yet fully reached consumer shelves

PPI caught the pressure early; CPI is still catching up.

## 4. What This Could Mean for the Rest of 2026

This isn’t necessarily a one-off. Possible implications:

1. **Pass-through risk is materializing** — Wider wholesale margins frequently translate into higher retail prices 3–9 months later. If services margins stay elevated, core CPI could re-accelerate mid-to-late 2026.

2. **Fed likely stays on hold longer** — The January PPI print reduced confidence in a smooth return to 2%. June 2026 rate cut odds fell; September became the new base case for many desks.

3. **Tariff prelude?** — Early tariff signaling in 2026 seems to be showing up first as margin expansion rather than raw goods inflation — a pattern that could keep core services sticky even if goods prices remain soft.

4. **Two-speed inflation persists** — Goods disinflation continues (positive for real incomes), but services re-accelerates (bad for the 2% target and monetary policy calibration).

Bottom line: January 2026 reminded markets that disinflation is bumpy and non-linear. The PPI services surge was a loud signal that underlying pricing pressures — especially in the sticky services sector — have not disappeared.
